# Diagram Catalog — Every Visual by Unit and Topic

This document catalogs every diagram in the APES Visual Exam Atlas, organized by type. Each entry references the topic number, topic title, diagram type, and what the diagram shows.

**Total diagrams:** 4 cycle diagrams + 9 terrestrial biome cards + 9 aquatic biome cards + 8 graph families + 8 pollution pathways + 10 energy source comparisons + 20 cause-effect chains = **68 primary diagrams** (plus 103 topic card visuals)

---

## 1. Cycle Diagrams (4)

The four major biogeochemical cycles. Each diagram shows reservoirs (storage pools), fluxes (movement pathways), and human disruptions.

| # | Cycle | Topic | LO | Reservoirs | Key Fluxes | Human Disruption | AP Trap |
|---|-------|-------|----|-----------|------------|------------------|---------|
| 1 | **Carbon Cycle** | 1.6 | ERT-1.F | Atmosphere (CO₂), biosphere (biomass), hydrosphere (dissolved CO₂/carbonate), lithosphere (fossil fuels, limestone) | Photosynthesis, respiration, combustion, decomposition, ocean absorption, sedimentation | Fossil fuel combustion transfers C from lithosphere to atmosphere faster than natural sinks can absorb | Students forget the ocean is the largest active carbon sink, and that limestone/fossil fuels are the largest long-term reservoir |
| 2 | **Nitrogen Cycle** | 1.7 | ERT-1.G | Atmosphere (N₂ — 78%), soil (NH₄⁺, NO₃⁻), biomass (amino acids, proteins), aquatic sediments | Nitrogen fixation (N₂ → NH₃), nitrification (NH₃ → NO₃⁻), assimilation, ammonification, denitrification (NO₃⁻ → N₂) | Haber-Bosch process doubles reactive nitrogen; fertilizer runoff causes eutrophication | Students confuse nitrogen fixation (N₂ → NH₃, done by bacteria) with nitrification (NH₃ → NO₃⁻); also forget atmospheric N₂ is NOT directly usable by most organisms |
| 3 | **Phosphorus Cycle** | 1.8 | ERT-1.H | Rocks/sediments (largest reservoir), soil, water, organisms | Weathering of rocks, uptake by plants, consumption by animals, decomposition, sedimentation | Mining phosphate rock for fertilizer; runoff causes eutrophication | Phosphorus has NO significant atmospheric phase — this is the #1 tested distinction; it is the slowest biogeochemical cycle |
| 4 | **Hydrologic (Water) Cycle** | 1.9 | ERT-1.I | Oceans (96.5%), glaciers/ice caps (1.74%), groundwater (1.69%), surface freshwater (0.01%), atmosphere (0.001%) | Evaporation, transpiration, condensation, precipitation, infiltration, runoff, groundwater flow | Impervious surfaces increase runoff and decrease infiltration; over-extraction of groundwater (aquifer depletion) | Students forget transpiration is a major flux; also confuse infiltration (water entering soil) with percolation (water moving through soil to groundwater) |

### Cycle Diagram Elements (what to include when drawing)
For each cycle, the atlas diagram includes:
- **Reservoirs** as labeled boxes/pools with relative size indicators
- **Flux arrows** with labels showing the process name
- **Human disruption** highlighted in a contrasting color
- **Residence time** annotations (how long matter stays in each reservoir)
- **Connection to other cycles** (e.g., carbon cycle connects to hydrologic cycle via ocean CO₂ dissolution)

## 2. Terrestrial Biome Cards (9)

Each biome card shows the defining abiotic factors, characteristic species, NPP range, and the climatograph pattern.

| # | Biome | Topic | Temperature Range | Precipitation Range | Characteristic Species | NPP (g/m²/yr) | Key Feature |
|---|-------|-------|-------------------|--------------------|-----------------------|----------------|-------------|
| 1 | **Tropical Rainforest** | 1.4 | 25–28°C year-round | 200–450 cm/yr | Kapok tree, toucan, bromeliad epiphytes, poison dart frog | 1,000–3,500 | Highest terrestrial biodiversity; nutrient-poor laterite soil (nutrients in biomass, not soil) |
| 2 | **Temperate Deciduous Forest** | 1.4 | −5 to 30°C (seasonal) | 75–150 cm/yr | Oak, maple, white-tailed deer, eastern bluebird | 600–2,500 | Distinct seasons; leaves drop in fall; rich humus-laden soil |
| 3 | **Temperate Grassland** | 1.4 | −20 to 30°C (extreme range) | 25–75 cm/yr | Bison, prairie dog, big bluestem grass, ferruginous hawk | 200–1,500 | Too dry for trees, too wet for desert; deep fertile mollisol soil; most converted to agriculture |
| 4 | **Taiga (Boreal Forest)** | 1.4 | −40 to 20°C | 30–85 cm/yr | Spruce, fir, moose, lynx, snowshoe hare | 400–2,000 | Largest terrestrial biome by area; acidic needle-litter soil; permafrost in northern sections |
| 5 | **Tundra** | 1.4 | −40 to 12°C | 15–25 cm/yr | Caribou, arctic fox, lichen, moss, sedge | 10–400 | Permafrost prevents tree growth; very short growing season; extremely fragile (slow recovery from disturbance) |
| 6 | **Desert** | 1.4 | 0–50°C (hot) or −25 to 25°C (cold) | <25 cm/yr | Saguaro cactus, kangaroo rat, sidewinder rattlesnake, creosote bush | 0–250 | <25 cm precipitation per year defines a desert; organisms have extreme water conservation adaptations (CAM photosynthesis) |
| 7 | **Savanna** | 1.4 | 20–30°C | 50–130 cm/yr (seasonal) | Acacia tree, elephant, zebra, lion, termite mounds | 200–2,000 | Grassland with scattered trees; distinct wet/dry seasons; fire-maintained (fire prevents forest encroachment) |
| 8 | **Chaparral (Mediterranean)** | 1.4 | 10–40°C | 25–60 cm/yr (winter rain) | Manzanita, scrub oak, coyote, California quail | 250–1,500 | Hot dry summers, mild wet winters; fire-adapted (serotinous cones, thick bark); found at ~30–40° latitude |
| 9 | **Temperate Rainforest** | 1.4 | 5–15°C | 200–350 cm/yr | Sitka spruce, western red cedar, banana slug, spotted owl | 1,000–3,500 | Extremely high moisture; epiphytes and mosses coat surfaces; found on western coastlines (Pacific NW, Chile, New Zealand) |

### Biome Diagram Elements
Each biome card in the atlas includes:
- **Climatograph** (bar chart for precipitation + line for temperature overlaid)
- **Characteristic organism illustrations** (plants and animals)
- **Soil profile** cross-section showing horizon characteristics
- **NPP bar** showing relative productivity compared to other biomes
- **Human impact annotation** (e.g., conversion to agriculture, deforestation rate)
- **Latitude/location band** on a world map

## 3. Aquatic Biome Cards (9)

Each aquatic zone card shows the defining abiotic factors, characteristic species, productivity, and human impacts.

| # | Zone/Biome | Topic | Salinity | Light | Characteristic Species | Productivity | Key Feature |
|---|-----------|-------|----------|-------|----------------------|-------------|-------------|
| 1 | **Coral Reef** | 1.5 | Marine (35 ppt) | Photic (shallow, clear) | Brain coral, parrotfish, clownfish, zooxanthellae | Very high | Highest aquatic biodiversity; built by cnidarians with mutualistic zooxanthellae; threatened by bleaching and acidification |
| 2 | **Open Ocean (Pelagic)** | 1.5 | Marine (35 ppt) | Photic zone thin; vast aphotic | Phytoplankton, tuna, jellyfish, whale | Low per area; high total | Low NPP per unit area (nutrient-poor); largest biome on Earth; contributes ~50% of global O₂ production due to sheer size |
| 3 | **Estuary** | 1.5 | Brackish (0.5–30 ppt, variable) | Photic (shallow) | Marsh grass (Spartina), blue crab, oyster, juvenile fish | Very high | Where freshwater rivers meet saltwater ocean; nursery habitat for 75%+ of commercial fish species; extreme salinity variation |
| 4 | **Freshwater Lake (Littoral/Limnetic/Profundal)** | 1.5 | Fresh (<0.5 ppt) | Varies by depth/zone | Bass, perch, lily pad, zooplankton, benthic invertebrates | Moderate–High | Zonation: littoral (near-shore, rooted plants), limnetic (open water, phytoplankton), profundal (deep, dark, decomposers) |
| 5 | **Freshwater Stream/River** | 1.5 | Fresh (<0.5 ppt) | Variable | Trout (headwaters), catfish (lower river), caddisfly larvae, algae | Low–Moderate | Defined by flow; organisms adapted to current; headwaters = cold, high DO, low nutrients; mouth = warm, low DO, high nutrients |
| 6 | **Wetland (Marsh/Swamp/Bog)** | 1.5 | Fresh or brackish | Photic (shallow) | Cattails, sphagnum moss, herons, amphibians | High | Water filtration, flood control, carbon storage, nursery habitat; marsh = herbaceous, swamp = trees, bog = acidic/sphagnum |
| 7 | **Mangrove Forest** | 1.5 | Brackish to marine | Photic (intertidal) | Red mangrove, manatee, various fish juveniles, crabs | High | Salt-tolerant trees with prop roots; coastal storm protection, sediment trapping, nursery habitat; tropical coastlines |
| 8 | **Deep Ocean (Abyssal/Benthic)** | 1.5 | Marine (35 ppt) | Aphotic (no light) | Tube worms, anglerfish, chemosynthetic bacteria, giant squid | Very low | No photosynthesis; food from marine snow (sinking organic matter) or chemosynthesis at hydrothermal vents; extreme pressure |
| 9 | **Intertidal Zone** | 1.5 | Marine (35 ppt) | Photic (exposed at low tide) | Barnacles, sea stars, mussels, anemones, kelp | Moderate–High | Organisms must tolerate wave action, desiccation, and temperature extremes; distinct vertical zonation (splash, high, mid, low) |

### Aquatic Diagram Elements
Each aquatic zone card includes:
- **Cross-section diagram** showing zones (photic/aphotic, littoral/limnetic/profundal)
- **Abiotic factor gradients** (light, temperature, dissolved oxygen, salinity)
- **Characteristic organism placement** within the diagram
- **Productivity indicator** relative to other aquatic zones
- **Human impact annotation** (e.g., eutrophication, thermal pollution, ocean acidification)

## 4. Graph Families (8)

The 8 graph types that appear most frequently on the AP exam. Each is implemented as an interactive simulator.

| # | Graph Family | Topics | X-Axis | Y-Axis | Key Shape/Pattern | AP Interpretation |
|---|-------------|--------|--------|--------|-------------------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Exponential Growth (J-curve)** | 3.5 | Time | Population size | J-shaped curve; accelerating upward | Population growing without limiting factors; dN/dt = rN; no population grows exponentially forever |
| 2 | **Logistic Growth (S-curve)** | 3.5 | Time | Population size | S-shaped curve leveling at K | Population growing with density-dependent limiting factors; dN/dt = rN((K−N)/K); levels off at carrying capacity (K) |
| 3 | **Survivorship Curves (Types I, II, III)** | 3.3 | Age (% of max lifespan) | Number surviving (log scale) | Type I: convex (humans), Type II: linear (birds), Type III: concave (oysters) | Type I: low early mortality, high late mortality (K-selected); Type III: high early mortality, low late mortality (r-selected) |
| 4 | **Age Structure Diagrams** | 3.6 | Population count (left: male, right: female) | Age cohort | Pyramid = rapid growth; Column = stable; Inverted = declining | Wide base = high birth rate (pre-reproductive > reproductive); narrow base = aging population (Japan, Italy) |
| 5 | **Dissolved Oxygen Sag Curve** | 8.5, 8.6 | Distance downstream from pollution source | Dissolved oxygen (mg/L) | Sharp drop after source, gradual recovery | DO drops as decomposers consume organic waste; recovers downstream as reaeration exceeds decomposition; clean zone → decomposition zone → septic zone → recovery zone → clean zone |
| 6 | **Dose-Response Curve** | 8.12 | Dose (concentration or amount) | Response (% affected) | Sigmoidal (S-shaped); marked at LD50/ED50 | LD50 = dose lethal to 50% of test population; threshold = dose below which no response; used to set safety standards |
| 7 | **Species-Area Curve** | 2.3 | Area (log scale) | Number of species (log scale) | Log-linear positive relationship | Larger islands/habitat patches support more species; used in island biogeography; 90% habitat loss ≈ 50% species loss |
| 8 | **Keeling Curve / CO₂ Trend** | 9.3, 9.4 | Year (1960–present) | Atmospheric CO₂ (ppm) | Upward trend with seasonal sawtooth oscillation | Annual oscillation = Northern Hemisphere seasonal photosynthesis/respiration; long-term rise = fossil fuel combustion; pre-industrial ~280 ppm → current ~420 ppm |

### Graph Diagram Elements
Each graph in the atlas includes:
- **Interactive data points** with hover values
- **Axis labels** with units
- **Trend line** with description
- **AP-safe interpretation** (what to write on the exam)
- **Common wrong interpretation** (what NOT to write)
- **Related FRQ sentence** (model answer for graph-interpretation FRQ)
- **Possible calculation** (e.g., percent change, rate, LD50 identification)

## 5. Pollution Pathways (8)

Each pathway traces a pollutant from source to environmental impact to solution.

| # | Pathway | Topic | Source Type | Pollutant(s) | Transport | Impact | Solution |
|---|---------|-------|------------|-------------|-----------|--------|----------|
| 1 | **Fertilizer → Eutrophication → Dead Zone** | 8.5 | Nonpoint | Nitrate, phosphate | Surface runoff to waterways | Algal bloom → decomposition → hypoxia → fish kill | Buffer strips, reduce fertilizer, wetland restoration |
| 2 | **Fossil Fuel → Acid Rain → Forest/Aquatic Decline** | 7.7 | Point (power plants) + Nonpoint (vehicles) | SO₂, NOₓ | Atmospheric transport (can travel 100s of miles) | pH < 5.6 precipitation; soil nutrient leaching; aquatic acidification | Scrubbers, catalytic converters, Clean Air Act |
| 3 | **Vehicle Exhaust → Photochemical Smog → Respiratory Illness** | 7.2 | Nonpoint (vehicles) | NOₓ, VOCs → ground-level O₃, PANs | UV-driven photochemical reactions in atmosphere | Asthma, bronchitis, crop damage, reduced visibility | Catalytic converters, public transit, emission standards |
| 4 | **Persistent Toxin → Bioaccumulation → Biomagnification** | 8.8 | Point + Nonpoint | DDT, mercury, PCBs | Absorbed by organisms; moves up food chain | Toxin concentration increases at each trophic level; top predators most affected | Ban persistent chemicals, biomonitoring, reduce point sources |
| 5 | **Power Plant Cooling → Thermal Pollution → Aquatic Stress** | 8.6 | Point (power plant outfall) | Heat (no chemical added) | Heated water discharged to river/lake | Reduced dissolved oxygen; cold-water species (trout) stressed/killed | Cooling towers, cooling ponds, discharge regulations |
| 6 | **Mining → Acid Mine Drainage → Water Contamination** | 5.9 | Point (mine site) | Sulfuric acid, heavy metals (As, Pb, Hg) | Acid mine drainage to surface and groundwater | Contaminated waterways, aquatic habitat destruction, soil contamination | Reclamation, water treatment, SMCRA regulation |
| 7 | **Urbanization → Impervious Surfaces → Runoff Pollution** | 5.10, 5.13 | Nonpoint (urban) | Oil, sediment, nutrients, trash, thermal runoff | Rapid surface runoff from roads, parking lots, roofs | Flooding, erosion, combined sewer overflows, urban heat island | Permeable pavement, green roofs, rain gardens, retention ponds |
| 8 | **CFC Release → Ozone Depletion → UV Damage** | 9.1 | Point (historically: aerosol, refrigerant manufacturing) | CFCs (chlorofluorocarbons) | CFCs rise to stratosphere over years | Cl· radical catalyzes O₃ destruction; increased UV-B at surface; skin cancer, crop damage, phytoplankton decline | Montreal Protocol (1987) banned CFC production |

### Pollution Pathway Diagram Elements
Each pathway diagram includes:
- **Source** (labeled with point vs. nonpoint classification)
- **Pollutant** (chemical name and formula where applicable)
- **Transport arrows** showing how the pollutant moves through the environment
- **Impact zone** showing environmental and/or health effects
- **Solution box** with mechanism of action
- **Tradeoff annotation** (every solution has a cost or limitation)

## 6. Energy Source Comparisons (10)

Each energy source card provides a standardized comparison including efficiency, environmental impacts, and math connections.

| # | Energy Source | Topic | Type | Efficiency | Advantages | Disadvantages | Environmental Impact | Math Connection |
|---|-------------|-------|------|-----------|-----------|---------------|---------------------|----------------|
| 1 | **Coal** | 6.5 | Nonrenewable (fossil fuel) | ~33% thermal | Abundant, cheap, reliable baseload | Highest CO₂/kWh, SO₂, NOₓ, particulates, mercury, ash, mountaintop removal mining | Acid rain, climate change, respiratory disease, habitat destruction | Calculate CO₂ emissions per kWh; percent change in coal consumption |
| 2 | **Oil (Petroleum)** | 6.5 | Nonrenewable (fossil fuel) | ~30% (vehicle) | High energy density, versatile (transport, plastics, heating) | CO₂ emissions, oil spills, geopolitical dependence, finite supply | Climate change, ocean pollution, habitat fragmentation from extraction | EROEI (Energy Return on Energy Invested) calculations |
| 3 | **Natural Gas** | 6.5 | Nonrenewable (fossil fuel) | ~45% (combined cycle) | Lowest CO₂ of fossil fuels, efficient, quick-start peaking plant | Still emits CO₂; fracking causes groundwater contamination, induced seismicity; methane leaks (CH₄ = 80x CO₂ over 20 yr) | Climate change (less than coal per kWh), water pollution from fracking | Compare CO₂ per kWh across fossil fuels |
| 4 | **Nuclear** | 6.6 | Nonrenewable (uranium) | ~33% thermal | No CO₂ during generation; extremely high energy density; reliable baseload | Radioactive waste (thousands of years); meltdown risk; high construction cost; uranium mining | Thermal pollution from cooling water; radioactive waste storage; mining impacts | Half-life calculations; energy density comparisons |
| 5 | **Solar (PV and Thermal)** | 6.8 | Renewable | ~20% (PV) ~40% (CSP) | No emissions during operation; modular; decreasing costs | Intermittent (no sun at night); energy storage needed; manufacturing uses rare materials; land use | Manufacturing waste (cadmium, silicon); land use for utility-scale; habitat disruption | Calculate panel area needed for a given power demand; capacity factor |
| 6 | **Wind** | 6.12 | Renewable | ~35–45% (capacity factor) | No emissions during operation; low water use; land can be dual-used (farming + turbines) | Intermittent; bird/bat mortality; noise; visual impact; transmission from remote sites | Bird and bat kills; noise pollution; visual landscape impact | Calculate number of turbines needed; percent change in wind capacity |
| 7 | **Hydroelectric** | 6.9 | Renewable | ~90% (highest of any source) | No emissions during operation; highest efficiency; reservoir = recreation; flood control | Fish migration blocked; sediment trapped; communities displaced; methane from reservoirs | Habitat disruption; altered river flow; reservoir methane (especially tropical); downstream erosion | Calculate energy from flow rate and head height; percent change in dam output |
| 8 | **Geothermal** | 6.10 | Renewable | ~15% (electricity) ~50–70% (direct use) | Baseload (24/7); small land footprint; low emissions | Limited to tectonically active areas; H₂S emissions; may deplete if over-extracted; high upfront cost | H₂S and CO₂ emissions (much less than fossil fuels); potential for induced seismicity; mineral-rich wastewater | Compare capacity factor to solar and wind |
| 9 | **Biomass / Biofuels** | 6.7 | Renewable (if sustainably harvested) | ~20–25% (electricity) | Carbon-neutral if regrown; uses waste materials; domestic source | Competes with food production; deforestation for plantations; still produces air pollutants when burned | Air pollution (particulates, CO, NOₓ); land use change; water use for crop-based biofuels | Calculate carbon neutrality math (CO₂ released = CO₂ absorbed during growth) |
| 10 | **Hydrogen Fuel Cells** | 6.11 | Depends on H₂ source | ~60% (fuel cell) | Only byproduct is water; high efficiency; versatile (vehicles, buildings) | H₂ production is energy-intensive; most H₂ from natural gas (grey hydrogen); storage challenges; expensive | If H₂ from electrolysis with renewables (green hydrogen): very low impact; if from natural gas: CO₂ emissions at production | Compare green vs. grey vs. blue hydrogen lifecycle emissions |

### Energy Source Diagram Elements
Each energy source card includes:
- **Source-to-electricity flow diagram** (extraction → processing → generation → transmission → consumption)
- **Efficiency graphic** (energy in vs. useful energy out)
- **Environmental impact icons** (CO₂, water use, land use, waste)
- **Comparison bar** showing relative values across all 10 sources
- **Math problem** connected to the source (calculation practice)

## 7. Cause-Effect Chains (20)

Each chain is a multi-step visual flow: Cause → Mechanism → Effect → Consequence → Solution → Tradeoff.

| # | Chain Title | Topic | Unit | Steps | Solution | Tradeoff |
|---|-----------|-------|------|-------|----------|----------|
| 1 | Fertilizer Runoff → Eutrophication → Dead Zone | 8.5 | 8 | Fertilizer → nitrate/phosphate runoff → algal bloom → sunlight blocked → decomposers consume DO → hypoxia → fish kill | Buffer strips, reduce fertilizer, wetland restoration | Lower crop yield or added cost for farmers |
| 2 | Fossil Fuel Combustion → Enhanced Greenhouse Effect → Climate Change | 9.4 | 9 | Combustion → CO₂ released → CO₂ absorbs IR → enhanced greenhouse effect → temperature rise → sea level rise, ice melt, extreme weather; positive feedback: permafrost thaw releases CH₄ | Renewable energy, carbon tax, efficiency standards, reforestation | Economic disruption, job losses in fossil fuel sector |
| 3 | CFCs → Ozone Depletion → Increased UV Radiation | 9.1 | 9 | CFCs released → rise to stratosphere → UV breaks CFC → Cl· released → Cl· catalyzes O₃ breakdown → ozone layer thins → increased UV-B → skin cancer, cataracts, crop damage | Montreal Protocol (1987) | Replacement HFCs are potent greenhouse gases |
| 4 | Mining → Habitat Destruction → Water Pollution | 5.9 | 5 | Mining operation → overburden removed → sulfide minerals exposed → acid mine drainage → contaminated waterways → habitat destruction | Reclamation, water treatment, SMCRA | Reclamation is expensive and may not restore original ecosystem |
| 5 | Urbanization → Impervious Surfaces → Flooding/Pollution | 5.10 | 5 | Urban development → impervious surfaces → rapid runoff → flooding, erosion, CSOs → oil/sediment/nutrients carried to waterways → urban heat island | Permeable pavement, green roofs, rain gardens | Higher construction costs, maintenance requirements |
| 6 | Deforestation → Carbon Release → Enhanced Greenhouse Effect | 1.6 | 1 | Trees cleared → stored carbon released as CO₂ → carbon sink removed → increased atmospheric CO₂ → enhanced greenhouse effect | Reforestation, sustainable forestry, REDD+ | Reduces agricultural land, economic costs for developing nations |
| 7 | Fossil Fuel Combustion → SO₂/NOₓ → Acid Rain | 7.7 | 7 | Coal plants/vehicles → SO₂ and NOₓ released → react with water → H₂SO₄ and HNO₃ → acid precipitation (pH < 5.6) → aquatic acidification, soil nutrient leaching | Scrubbers, catalytic converters, Clean Air Act | Scrubbers produce waste sludge; catalytic converters require rare metals |
| 8 | Vehicle Emissions → Photochemical Smog → Respiratory Illness | 7.2 | 7 | Vehicle exhaust → NOₓ and VOCs → UV drives photochemical reactions → ground-level O₃ and PANs → respiratory problems, crop damage | Catalytic converters, public transit, emission standards | Vehicle emission controls increase vehicle cost |
| 9 | Persistent Toxin → Bioaccumulation → Biomagnification | 8.8 | 8 | DDT/mercury/PCBs released → absorbed by organisms → bioaccumulation in fatty tissues → predator eats contaminated prey → biomagnification → top predators highest concentration | Ban persistent chemicals, reduce point-source pollution | Replacement chemicals may have unknown long-term effects |
| 10 | CO₂ Absorption → Ocean Acidification → Coral/Shell Decline | 9.7 | 9 | Atmospheric CO₂ increases → ocean absorbs ~30% → CO₂ + H₂O → carbonic acid → pH drops → reduced carbonate ions → shell/coral organisms can't build CaCO₃ | Reduce CO₂ emissions, marine protected areas | Even with emission reductions, pH recovery takes centuries |
| 11 | Species Introduction → Invasive Spread → Ecosystem Disruption | 9.8 | 9 | Non-native species introduced → no natural predators → rapid population growth → outcompetes natives → ecosystem structure altered | Early detection, removal, trade regulations, biological control | Biological control agents may themselves become invasive |
| 12 | Pesticide Use → Resistance → Pesticide Treadmill | 5.6 | 5 | Broad-spectrum pesticide → most pests killed, resistant survive → resistant population → farmer applies more/stronger chemical → pesticide treadmill → ecosystem contamination | IPM: biological control, crop rotation, targeted application | IPM is more labor-intensive and requires monitoring |
| 13 | Power Plant Cooling Water → Thermal Pollution → Aquatic Stress | 8.6 | 8 | Power plant uses cooling water → heated water discharged → water temperature rises → DO decreases → cold-water species stressed/killed | Cooling towers, cooling ponds, discharge regulations | Cooling towers are expensive and use water for evaporation |
| 14 | Population Growth → Resource Depletion → Environmental Degradation | 3.5 | 3 | Population grows → increased demand for food/water/energy/land → intensified agriculture, deforestation, fossil fuel extraction → habitat loss, soil degradation, climate change | Family planning, education, sustainable development | Development programs require funding; cultural barriers |
| 15 | Disturbance → Ecological Succession → Climax Community | 2.7 | 2 | Major disturbance → pioneer species colonize → soil builds, conditions change → species diversity increases → climax community | Prescribed burns maintain some ecosystems in earlier stages | Succession takes decades to centuries |
| 16 | Dam Construction → Altered River Ecosystem | 6.9 | 6 | Dam built → river flow altered, reservoir created → fish migration blocked → sediment trapped → downstream erosion → reservoir methane | Fish ladders, run-of-river designs, dam removal | Fish ladders have limited effectiveness; dam removal costly |
| 17 | Overgrazing → Desertification | 5.4 | 5 | Livestock overgrazing → vegetation removed faster than regrowth → soil exposed → topsoil lost to erosion → land becomes barren → desertification | Rotational grazing, reduced stocking, revegetation | Reduced herd size impacts ranchers' livelihoods |
| 18 | Ocean Warming → Coral Bleaching → Reef Collapse | 9.6 | 9 | GHG emissions → ocean absorbs heat → sea surface temperature rises → coral expels zooxanthellae → bleached coral → prolonged bleaching → death → reef collapse | Reduce emissions, marine protected areas, coral restoration | Warming may outpace coral adaptation even with protection |
| 19 | Irrigation → Salinization → Crop Yield Decline | 5.5 | 5 | Flood irrigation in arid region → water evaporates → salts left in soil → salt concentration rises → osmotic stress on plants → crop yield decline → land abandonment | Drip irrigation, soil flushing, salt-tolerant crops | Drip irrigation has higher upfront cost; flushing uses more water |
| 20 | Nuclear Fission → Radioactive Waste → Long-term Storage Challenge | 6.6 | 6 | Uranium fission → heat + radioactive byproducts → spent fuel rods radioactive for thousands of years → requires deep geologic repository → risk of contamination | Dry cask storage, vitrification, deep geologic repository | No community wants a repository nearby (NIMBY) |

### Cause-Effect Chain Diagram Elements
Each chain diagram includes:
- **Sequential flow boxes** color-coded by step type (cause = red, mechanism = orange, effect = yellow, consequence = purple, solution = green, tradeoff = gray)
- **Arrows** connecting each step
- **Feedback loop annotations** where applicable (e.g., permafrost thaw → CH₄ → more warming)
- **Stakeholder callouts** identifying who is affected
- **FRQ connection** showing how this chain maps to AP task verbs
