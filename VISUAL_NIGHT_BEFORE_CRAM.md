# Night-Before Cram — 50 Highest-Yield Visual Concepts

Each entry has one **must-know sentence** and one **AP trap** (the mistake that costs you points). Read the sentence, then check yourself against the trap. If you hesitate on ANY trap, go back and study that topic.

Ordered by exam weight and frequency of appearance on released AP exams.

## Unit 9 — Global Change (15–20% of exam) → 10 concepts

### 1. Enhanced Greenhouse Effect
**Must-know:** Greenhouse gases (CO₂, CH₄, N₂O) absorb and re-emit infrared radiation in the troposphere, and human combustion of fossil fuels has increased atmospheric CO₂ from ~280 ppm (pre-industrial) to ~420 ppm, enhancing this natural effect.
**AP trap:** The greenhouse effect itself is natural and necessary for life — without it Earth would be −18°C. The problem is the ENHANCED greenhouse effect from added GHGs. Don't say "the greenhouse effect is bad."

### 2. Ozone Depletion ≠ Climate Change
**Must-know:** Ozone depletion is caused by CFCs breaking down O₃ in the stratosphere (increasing UV radiation), while climate change is caused by greenhouse gases trapping IR in the troposphere (increasing temperature) — they are completely different problems with different causes and different effects.
**AP trap:** Greenhouse gases do NOT destroy the ozone layer. CFCs destroy ozone. CO₂ traps heat. Different chemicals, different atmospheric layers, different mechanisms.

### 3. Climate Change vs. Weather
**Must-know:** Climate is the 30+ year average of weather patterns in a region; climate change refers to statistically significant shifts in these long-term averages, not individual weather events.
**AP trap:** A single cold winter does NOT disprove climate change. Climate is long-term trends; weather is short-term variation.

### 4. Positive Feedback Loops (Climate)
**Must-know:** Ice-albedo feedback (melting ice → less reflection → more absorption → more melting) and permafrost thaw (warming → permafrost melts → releases CH₄ → more warming) amplify climate change beyond initial CO₂ forcing.
**AP trap:** "Positive feedback" does NOT mean "good" — it means the effect amplifies itself. Positive feedbacks accelerate climate change.

### 5. Ocean Acidification Mechanism
**Must-know:** Ocean absorbs ~30% of atmospheric CO₂; CO₂ + H₂O → carbonic acid (H₂CO₃) → lower pH → reduced carbonate ion availability → corals and shell-forming organisms cannot build CaCO₃ structures.
**AP trap:** The ocean is NOT becoming acidic (pH < 7) — it's becoming LESS basic. Current ocean pH is ~8.1, down from ~8.2. "Acidification" means the pH is dropping, not that it's crossed into acidic territory.

### 6. Coral Bleaching
**Must-know:** When ocean temperatures rise, stressed coral expels its mutualistic zooxanthellae algae, turning white (bleached) and losing its primary food source; prolonged bleaching leads to death and reef collapse.
**AP trap:** Corals are ANIMALS (phylum Cnidaria), not plants. The zooxanthellae are the photosynthetic algae. Bleaching doesn't instantly kill coral — recovery is possible if temperatures drop quickly.

### 7. Invasive Species Success
**Must-know:** Invasive species thrive in new environments because they lack natural predators, competitors, and diseases, enabling exponential population growth that displaces native species.
**AP trap:** Not all non-native species are invasive. A species is only invasive if it causes ecological or economic harm. Many non-native species coexist without significant impact.

### 8. HIPPCO Framework
**Must-know:** The six major threats to biodiversity are Habitat loss (currently #1), Invasive species, Population growth, Pollution, Climate change, and Overexploitation — remembered as HIPPCO.
**AP trap:** Habitat loss, NOT climate change, is currently the #1 driver of biodiversity loss. Climate change is projected to become dominant by mid-century but is not there yet.

### 9. Montreal Protocol Success
**Must-know:** The Montreal Protocol (1987) banned CFC production, allowing the ozone layer to begin recovering — it is the most successful international environmental agreement in history.
**AP trap:** HFC replacements for CFCs don't deplete ozone but ARE potent greenhouse gases. The Kigali Amendment (2016) now phases down HFCs. One solved problem created another.

### 10. Mitigation vs. Adaptation
**Must-know:** Mitigation reduces the cause of climate change (e.g., switching to renewable energy reduces GHG emissions), while adaptation adjusts to the effects (e.g., building sea walls for sea level rise).
**AP trap:** A sea wall is adaptation, NOT mitigation — it doesn't reduce emissions. Planting trees is mitigation (carbon sink). The AP exam tests this distinction in FRQ "propose a solution" prompts.

## Unit 3 — Populations (10–15%) → 6 concepts

### 11. Exponential vs. Logistic Growth
**Must-know:** Exponential growth (J-curve, dN/dt = rN) occurs without limiting factors; logistic growth (S-curve, dN/dt = rN((K−N)/K)) occurs when density-dependent factors slow growth as population approaches carrying capacity (K).
**AP trap:** No population grows exponentially forever. When the exam shows a J-curve, the follow-up question is always "what limits growth?" Answer: density-dependent factors.

### 12. Carrying Capacity Is Not Fixed
**Must-know:** Carrying capacity (K) is the maximum population an environment can sustain indefinitely, but K can change if environmental conditions change (drought, habitat loss, resource depletion, or habitat improvement).
**AP trap:** Students assume K is a permanent number. K shifts with changes in food availability, disease, habitat quality, and climate.

### 13. Survivorship Curves
**Must-know:** Type I (convex — humans): low early mortality, high late mortality. Type II (linear — birds): constant mortality rate. Type III (concave — oysters, sea turtles): extremely high early mortality, low late mortality for survivors.
**AP trap:** The Y-axis is LOGARITHMIC. On a log scale, Type II appears as a straight line. If the axes aren't labeled, students misidentify the curve shapes.

### 14. Age Structure Diagrams
**Must-know:** Wide base = rapid growth (high proportion pre-reproductive). Even column = stable (even distribution). Narrow base/inverted = declining (low proportion pre-reproductive, aging population).
**AP trap:** The base shows the PRE-REPRODUCTIVE population. A wide base means lots of individuals who haven't yet started reproducing — the population WILL grow even if birth rates drop (population momentum).

### 15. Demographic Transition
**Must-know:** Stage 1: high birth/high death. Stage 2: high birth/declining death (population boom). Stage 3: declining birth/low death (growth slows). Stage 4: low birth/low death (stable or declining).
**AP trap:** Stage 2 is where the population EXPLOSION occurs because death rates drop (better sanitation, medicine) while birth rates remain high. The biggest growth is NOT in Stage 1.

### 16. r-Selected vs. K-Selected
**Must-know:** r-selected species (bacteria, insects, weeds): many small offspring, little parental care, early reproduction, short lifespan, boom-bust cycles. K-selected species (elephants, whales, humans): few large offspring, high parental care, late reproduction, long lifespan, stable near K.
**AP trap:** This is a spectrum, not a binary. Most species fall somewhere in between. Salmon produce thousands of eggs (r-trait) but have a multi-year lifespan (K-trait).

## Unit 5 — Land and Water Use (10–15%) → 6 concepts

### 17. Tragedy of the Commons
**Must-know:** When a shared resource (commons) is available to all, each individual has incentive to maximize their own use, leading to overexploitation and resource depletion — individual rational behavior produces collectively irrational outcomes.
**AP trap:** The "commons" is a SHARED resource, not government-owned land. Examples: ocean fisheries, atmosphere (for pollution), public grazing land, groundwater aquifers.

### 18. Green Revolution Tradeoffs
**Must-know:** The Green Revolution (1960s–70s) dramatically increased crop yields through high-yield varieties, irrigation, synthetic fertilizers, and pesticides — preventing mass famine but increasing water use, soil degradation, chemical runoff, and monoculture vulnerability.
**AP trap:** The Green Revolution is not purely "good" or "bad." The AP exam expects you to acknowledge both the benefit (billions fed) and the environmental costs (eutrophication, soil depletion, groundwater depletion).

### 19. Irrigation and Salinization
**Must-know:** In arid regions, flood and furrow irrigation leads to salinization as water evaporates and leaves dissolved salts behind in the topsoil, eventually making the land unsuitable for agriculture.
**AP trap:** Drip irrigation (90–95% efficient) is the solution, but it has high upfront costs. The AP exam asks for BOTH the mechanism of salinization AND the solution with its tradeoff.

### 20. IPM (Integrated Pest Management)
**Must-know:** IPM combines biological control, crop rotation, habitat manipulation, and targeted (minimal) pesticide use to manage pests while minimizing chemical application and pesticide resistance.
**AP trap:** IPM does NOT mean zero pesticides. It means using pesticides as a LAST resort, in targeted amounts, combined with other strategies. "Eliminate all pesticides" is NOT the correct answer.

### 21. Pesticide Treadmill
**Must-know:** Broad-spectrum pesticides kill susceptible pests but select for resistant individuals, which reproduce and rebuild a resistant population, requiring stronger or more frequent pesticide application — an accelerating cycle.
**AP trap:** Pesticide resistance is EVOLUTIONARY (natural selection), not individual organisms becoming immune. The resistant individuals were already present as genetic variants; the pesticide selected for them.

### 22. Point Source vs. Nonpoint Source
**Must-know:** Point-source pollution comes from a single identifiable location (factory pipe, sewage outfall), while nonpoint-source pollution comes from many diffuse sources across a landscape (agricultural runoff, urban stormwater).
**AP trap:** Agricultural fields are NOT a point source even though you can identify the farm. Runoff from fields enters waterways from many points along the field edge — it's diffuse, making it nonpoint source.

## Unit 6 — Energy Resources and Consumption (10–15%) → 5 concepts

### 23. Nuclear: No CO₂ During Generation
**Must-know:** Nuclear fission produces electricity without emitting CO₂ during generation. The visible plume from cooling towers is WATER VAPOR (steam), not smoke or carbon dioxide.
**AP trap:** Students see cooling tower plumes and think "pollution." The byproduct is radioactive waste, NOT CO₂. Lifecycle emissions from mining/construction are very low compared to fossil fuels.

### 24. All Energy Sources Have Tradeoffs
**Must-know:** Every energy source — including renewables — has environmental impacts: solar (land use, manufacturing waste), wind (bird/bat mortality), hydro (habitat disruption, reservoir methane, blocked fish migration), biomass (air pollution, deforestation if not sustainably managed).
**AP trap:** "Renewable energy has no environmental impacts" will lose you points. The AP exam ALWAYS asks for disadvantages of renewables alongside advantages.

### 25. Fossil Fuel CO₂ Ranking
**Must-know:** CO₂ emissions per kWh: coal (highest) > oil > natural gas (lowest of fossil fuels). Natural gas produces ~50% less CO₂ than coal per kWh.
**AP trap:** Natural gas is STILL a fossil fuel that emits CO₂ and is nonrenewable. Its "clean" reputation is relative to coal, not absolute. Additionally, methane leaks during extraction (fracking) partially offset its CO₂ advantage — CH₄ has 80× the warming potential of CO₂ over 20 years.

### 26. Hydroelectric Efficiency vs. Impact
**Must-know:** Hydroelectric power has the highest efficiency of any energy source (~90%) and produces no direct emissions, but dams block fish migration, trap sediment, displace communities, and tropical reservoirs can release methane from decomposing vegetation.
**AP trap:** Dams are NOT impact-free. Fish ladders help but are not 100% effective. Some rivers are being restored by dam REMOVAL (Elwha River, WA).

### 27. Energy Conservation vs. Energy Efficiency
**Must-know:** Conservation means using LESS energy (turning off lights, driving less). Efficiency means getting MORE work from the SAME energy (LED bulbs, insulation, hybrid engines).
**AP trap:** These are different strategies. The AP exam asks you to distinguish them: "Drive less" = conservation. "Drive a more fuel-efficient car" = efficiency. Both reduce energy consumption but through different mechanisms.

## Unit 4 — Earth Systems and Resources (10–15%) → 4 concepts

### 28. Soil Horizons
**Must-know:** Soil horizons from top to bottom: O (organic matter/humus) → A (topsoil, richest in nutrients) → E (eluviation, leached zone) → B (subsoil, minerals accumulate) → C (weathered parent material) → R (bedrock).
**AP trap:** The A horizon (topsoil) is the most important for agriculture — it contains the most organic matter and nutrients. Erosion primarily removes the A horizon. Once lost, it takes 500–1,000 years to form 1 inch of topsoil.

### 29. Plate Tectonics and Resource Distribution
**Must-know:** Convergent boundaries (collision/subduction): volcanoes, earthquakes, mountain formation, mineral deposits. Divergent boundaries (spreading): mid-ocean ridges, new crust, geothermal. Transform boundaries (sliding): earthquakes.
**AP trap:** Geothermal energy is linked to plate boundaries. Iceland (divergent) and Pacific Ring of Fire (convergent) have geothermal resources. Don't suggest geothermal for areas far from plate boundaries.

### 30. El Niño / La Niña
**Must-know:** El Niño: weakened trade winds → warm water pools in eastern Pacific → wetter western Americas, drier western Pacific, reduced upwelling → less nutrient-rich water → fishery collapse. La Niña: strengthened trade winds → opposite pattern.
**AP trap:** El Niño is a NATURAL climate oscillation, not caused by climate change (though climate change may affect its frequency/intensity). Don't confuse ENSO with anthropogenic global warming.

### 31. Watersheds
**Must-know:** A watershed is all the land area that drains to a common water body, defined by topographic divides. Pollution anywhere in a watershed affects the waterway at the bottom — this is why nonpoint-source pollution is so hard to control.
**AP trap:** Upstream activities affect downstream water quality. A factory 50 miles upstream IS relevant to water quality at the river mouth. The AP exam loves questions connecting watershed land use to water quality.

## Unit 1 — The Living World: Ecosystems (6–8%) → 8 concepts

### 32. Energy Flows, Matter Cycles
**Must-know:** Energy enters ecosystems as sunlight, flows ONE WAY through trophic levels (losing ~90% as heat at each step), and exits as heat. Matter (C, N, P, H₂O) CYCLES between organisms and the abiotic environment through biogeochemical cycles.
**AP trap:** NEVER say "energy cycles." NEVER say "matter flows one way." Energy flows. Matter cycles. This is the most fundamental distinction in APES.

### 33. The 10% Rule
**Must-know:** Only ~10% of energy at one trophic level is transferred to the next; ~90% is lost as heat through cellular respiration. This is why ecosystems rarely support more than 4–5 trophic levels.
**AP trap:** If a plant captures 10,000 kcal, only ~1,000 kcal reaches primary consumers, ~100 kcal reaches secondary consumers, and ~10 kcal reaches tertiary consumers. The exam asks you to calculate this.

### 34. Carbon Cycle — Human Disruption
**Must-know:** Fossil fuel combustion transfers carbon from the lithosphere (where it was locked for millions of years) to the atmosphere much faster than natural processes can re-absorb it, increasing atmospheric CO₂.
**AP trap:** The ocean is the largest ACTIVE carbon sink, but the lithosphere (fossil fuels + limestone) is the largest TOTAL carbon reservoir. Students confuse "active sink" with "largest reservoir."

### 35. Nitrogen Cycle — Fixation vs. Nitrification
**Must-know:** Nitrogen fixation (N₂ → NH₃/NH₄⁺) is done by nitrogen-fixing bacteria (Rhizobium in legume root nodules, cyanobacteria). Nitrification (NH₃ → NO₃⁻) is done by nitrifying bacteria. Denitrification (NO₃⁻ → N₂) returns nitrogen to the atmosphere.
**AP trap:** Atmospheric N₂ is NOT directly usable by most organisms. It must first be "fixed" into ammonium (NH₄⁺) or nitrate (NO₃⁻). The Haber-Bosch process is artificial nitrogen fixation for fertilizer.

### 36. Phosphorus — No Atmospheric Phase
**Must-know:** Unlike carbon and nitrogen, the phosphorus cycle has NO significant atmospheric component. Phosphorus cycles through rock weathering → soil → plants → animals → decomposition → sedimentation → rock formation. It is the slowest major biogeochemical cycle.
**AP trap:** When the exam asks "which biogeochemical cycle lacks an atmospheric component?" — the answer is always phosphorus.

### 37. GPP vs. NPP
**Must-know:** GPP = total energy fixed by photosynthesis. NPP = GPP − R (energy remaining after plant respiration). NPP is the energy available to consumers.
**AP trap:** Plants do BOTH photosynthesis AND cellular respiration. Don't say "only animals respire." During the day, photosynthesis exceeds respiration (net O₂ production). At night, only respiration occurs.

### 38. Biome Determination
**Must-know:** The two primary abiotic factors that determine a terrestrial biome are temperature and precipitation. A climatograph (temperature line + precipitation bars) uniquely identifies each biome.
**AP trap:** Deserts are defined by LOW PRECIPITATION (<25 cm/yr), not by heat. Antarctica is a desert. The Gobi Desert is a cold desert. "Desert" does NOT mean "hot."

### 39. Aquatic Productivity Paradox
**Must-know:** Open ocean has LOW net primary productivity per unit area (nutrient-poor surface waters) but contributes enormously to total global production because it covers ~70% of Earth's surface and produces ~50% of global oxygen.
**AP trap:** Don't say "the ocean is highly productive." Say "the ocean has low productivity per unit area but high TOTAL productivity due to its vast size."

## Unit 2 — Biodiversity (6–8%) → 4 concepts

### 40. Primary vs. Secondary Succession
**Must-know:** Primary succession starts on bare rock/new surface with NO soil (volcanic eruption, glacier retreat). Secondary succession starts where soil already exists but vegetation was removed (fire, farming, logging). Secondary is faster because soil is already present.
**AP trap:** Pioneer species differ: primary = lichens/mosses (they create soil from rock). Secondary = grasses/herbs (soil with seed bank already exists).

### 41. Species Richness vs. Evenness
**Must-know:** Biodiversity has two components: species richness (number of different species) and species evenness (relative abundance of each species). High biodiversity requires BOTH many species AND relatively equal populations.
**AP trap:** A forest with 100 trees: 97 oak + 1 maple + 1 elm + 1 hickory has HIGH richness (4 species) but LOW evenness (dominated by oak) = MODERATE biodiversity. A forest with 25 of each species has HIGH richness AND HIGH evenness = HIGH biodiversity.

### 42. Island Biogeography
**Must-know:** The theory of island biogeography predicts that larger islands and islands closer to the mainland will have more species, because larger area supports more habitats and proximity increases immigration rates.
**AP trap:** This theory applies to ALL habitat "islands" — not just ocean islands. A forest fragment surrounded by farmland, a mountaintop surrounded by desert, or a lake surrounded by land all follow the same pattern.

### 43. Ecological Tolerance
**Must-know:** Every organism has an optimal range, zones of physiological stress, and zones of intolerance for each abiotic factor (temperature, pH, salinity, dissolved oxygen). Outside the tolerance range, the organism cannot survive.
**AP trap:** A single abiotic factor at the limit of tolerance can be lethal even if all other factors are optimal. This is Liebig's law of the minimum — the most limiting factor controls growth.

## Unit 7 — Atmospheric Pollution (7–10%) → 4 concepts

### 44. Primary vs. Secondary Pollutants
**Must-know:** Primary pollutants are emitted directly from a source (CO, SO₂, NOₓ, particulates, VOCs). Secondary pollutants form from reactions of primary pollutants in the atmosphere (ground-level O₃, PANs, H₂SO₄, HNO₃).
**AP trap:** Ground-level ozone (O₃) is a SECONDARY pollutant formed from NOₓ + VOCs + UV light. It is NOT emitted directly. Don't say "cars emit ozone."

### 45. Acid Rain: SO₂ and NOₓ, Not CO₂
**Must-know:** Acid rain (acid deposition) is caused by SO₂ and NOₓ from fossil fuel combustion reacting with atmospheric water to form sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) and nitric acid (HNO₃).
**AP trap:** CO₂ dissolves in water to form weak carbonic acid but is NOT the cause of acid rain. Normal rain is already slightly acidic (pH ≈ 5.6) from dissolved CO₂. Acid rain has pH < 5.6 due to SO₂ and NOₓ.

### 46. Thermal Inversion
**Must-know:** A thermal inversion occurs when a layer of warm air sits above cool air near the surface, preventing vertical mixing and trapping pollutants near ground level, worsening air quality.
**AP trap:** The inversion TRAPS pollution; it doesn't CREATE it. The pollution was already being emitted — the inversion prevents it from dispersing. Cities in valleys (Los Angeles, Mexico City) are especially vulnerable.

### 47. Indoor Air Pollution
**Must-know:** Indoor air can be 2–5× more polluted than outdoor air. Key indoor pollutants: radon (radioactive gas from rock — #1 cause of lung cancer in non-smokers), CO (incomplete combustion), formaldehyde (building materials), asbestos (insulation), mold.
**AP trap:** Radon is colorless and odorless. It seeps up from uranium-containing bedrock. The only way to detect it is testing. Many students forget radon because it's invisible — the AP exam loves to test it.

## Unit 8 — Aquatic and Terrestrial Pollution (7–10%) → 3 concepts

### 48. Eutrophication — The Indirect Kill
**Must-know:** Eutrophication pathway: excess nutrients → algal bloom → algae die → bacteria decompose dead algae → bacteria consume dissolved oxygen → hypoxia (DO < 2 mg/L) → fish suffocate. Fertilizer doesn't directly poison fish — it's the OXYGEN DEPLETION.
**AP trap:** The fish don't die from the fertilizer. They die from lack of oxygen. The causal chain is indirect: fertilizer → algae → decomposition → oxygen depletion → fish kill. The AP exam requires all steps.

### 49. Bioaccumulation ≠ Biomagnification
**Must-know:** Bioaccumulation = toxin builds up in ONE individual over its lifetime. Biomagnification = toxin concentration INCREASES at each successive trophic level in a food chain. Biomagnification requires bioaccumulation first.
**AP trap:** DDT biomagnification example: water (0.000003 ppm) → zooplankton (0.04 ppm) → small fish (0.5 ppm) → large fish (2 ppm) → bald eagle (25 ppm). Top predators are most affected. Use specific numbers if you can.

### 50. LD50 and Dose-Response
**Must-know:** LD50 is the dose of a substance that kills 50% of a test population. A LOWER LD50 means the substance is MORE toxic (less is needed to kill). The dose-response curve is sigmoidal (S-shaped), with a threshold below which no response occurs.
**AP trap:** LD50 does NOT mean "50% of the dose is lethal." It means "the dose at which 50% of the test population dies." Lower LD50 = MORE toxic (botulinum toxin LD50 ≈ 0.001 mg/kg vs. table salt LD50 ≈ 3,000 mg/kg).

## Quick-Reference: The 8 Confusable Pairs (final review)

| # | Pair | Key Distinction |
|---|------|----------------|
| 1 | Ozone depletion vs. climate change | Different atmospheric layers (stratosphere vs troposphere), different causes (CFCs vs GHGs), different effects (UV vs temperature) |
| 2 | Bioaccumulation vs. biomagnification | Individual organism over time vs. food chain across trophic levels |
| 3 | Energy flow vs. matter cycling | Energy: one way, in as light, out as heat. Matter: cycles, recycled between biotic and abiotic |
| 4 | GPP vs. NPP | GPP = total photosynthesis. NPP = GPP − plant respiration = energy available to consumers |
| 5 | Primary vs. secondary succession | Primary: no soil (bare rock). Secondary: soil present (disturbance removed vegetation) |
| 6 | Point source vs. nonpoint source | Point: single identifiable discharge (pipe). Nonpoint: diffuse across landscape (runoff) |
| 7 | Weather vs. climate | Weather: short-term, local. Climate: 30+ year average, regional/global |
| 8 | Mitigation vs. adaptation | Mitigation: reduce the cause (cut emissions). Adaptation: cope with the effects (sea wall) |

## The Five Sentences That Score FRQ Points

If you can write these five sentences correctly on the exam, you will earn points on almost any FRQ:

1. **Energy sentence:** "Energy flows one way through ecosystems, entering as sunlight and exiting as heat, with approximately 10% transferred between trophic levels."

2. **Eutrophication sentence:** "Excess nitrogen and phosphorus from agricultural runoff stimulate algal growth; when algae die, decomposer bacteria consume dissolved oxygen, creating hypoxic conditions that cause fish kills."

3. **Carbon cycle sentence:** "Fossil fuel combustion transfers carbon that was stored in the lithosphere for millions of years to the atmosphere as CO₂, faster than natural sinks can absorb it, enhancing the greenhouse effect."

4. **Solution-tradeoff sentence:** "[Solution] works by [specific mechanism], which [specific advantage], but [specific disadvantage/tradeoff], and may unintentionally [unintended consequence]."

5. **Biodiversity sentence:** "Habitat loss is currently the primary driver of biodiversity decline, as agricultural expansion has converted 40% of ice-free land to cropland and pasture, fragmenting ecosystems and reducing both species richness and genetic diversity."
