# APES Visual Exam Atlas — User Guide

## What Is the Visual Exam Atlas?

The APES Visual Exam Atlas is an interactive, visual-first review tool for AP Environmental Science. It covers all 9 AP units (103 topic cards), organized into 13 navigation modes, with interactive diagrams, cause-effect chains, graph simulators, and exam-prep tools — all aligned to the official College Board Course and Exam Description (CED).

**URL:** `/enviro`

---

## Quick-Start Workflow

1. **Open the atlas** at the `/enviro` route.
2. **Choose a navigation mode** from the left sidebar (13 modes available).
3. **Search or filter** by keyword or unit using the top bar.
4. **Click any topic card** to open the side panel with full detail.
5. **Switch modes** to study the same content from different angles (e.g., view a cycle in "Cycles" mode, then see its cause-effect chain in "Cause & Effect" mode).

## The 13 Navigation Modes

### 1. Everything Map (`everything`)
**Icon:** Globe  
**Purpose:** Birds-eye view of all 103 topic cards across all 9 units.  
**How to use:** Scan the full landscape. Click any card to open its detail panel. Useful for finding gaps in your knowledge — if you can't summarize a card in one sentence, click in and study it.

### 2. By AP Unit (`units`)
**Icon:** BookOpen  
**Purpose:** Browse topics organized by their official AP unit (Units 1–9).  
**How to use:** Each unit section shows its exam weight, topic count, and all cards in that unit. Start with high-weight units (Unit 9: 15–20%, Unit 3/4/5/6: 10–15% each). Cards are shown in CED order.

**Unit weights at a glance:**

|------|-------|-------------|--------|
| 1 | The Living World: Ecosystems | 6–8% | 1.1–1.11 |
| 2 | The Living World: Biodiversity | 6–8% | 2.1–2.8 |
| 3 | Populations | 10–15% | 3.1–3.9 |
| 4 | Earth Systems and Resources | 10–15% | 4.1–4.9 |
| 5 | Land and Water Use | 10–15% | 5.1–5.17 |
| 6 | Energy Resources and Consumption | 10–15% | 6.1–6.13 |

### 3. Cycles (`cycles`)
**Icon:** RefreshCw  
**Purpose:** Focus on the 4 major biogeochemical cycles: Carbon, Nitrogen, Phosphorus, and Hydrologic.  
**How to use:** Each cycle diagram shows reservoirs, fluxes, and human disruptions. Study the reservoir (storage pool) vs. flux (movement) distinction — the AP exam tests this frequently. Pay special attention to which cycles have an atmospheric component (C, N, H₂O) and which do not (P).

### 4. Biomes (`biomes`)
**Icon:** Trees  
**Purpose:** Explore the 9 terrestrial biomes and 9 aquatic biome zones.  
**How to use:** Each biome card shows defining abiotic factors (temperature range, precipitation), characteristic species, net primary productivity, and human impacts. Use the comparison layout to contrast biomes (e.g., tundra vs. desert: both have low precipitation, but for different reasons).

### 5. Cause & Effect (`cause-effect`)
**Icon:** GitBranch  
**Purpose:** Walk through 20 multi-step cause-effect chains that cover the most-tested environmental problems.  
**How to use:** Each chain is displayed as a visual flow from cause → mechanism → effect → consequence → solution → tradeoff. These chains are FRQ gold — practice tracing a complete chain aloud in under 60 seconds. The tradeoff step at the end is critical for "evaluate" and "justify" FRQ prompts.

### 6. Graphs & Data (`graphs`)
**Icon:** BarChart3  
**Purpose:** Interactive graph simulator showing 8 graph families the AP exam commonly tests.  
**How to use:** Each graph shows axes, data points, trend description, AP-safe interpretation, and the common wrong interpretation. Practice reading the graph before revealing the answer. The graph families include population growth curves, survivorship curves, age structure diagrams, dissolved oxygen sag curves, dose-response curves, species-area curves, and climate data graphs.

### 7. Percent Change Lab (`percent-change`)
**Icon:** Calculator  
**Purpose:** Practice the percent change calculation that appears on nearly every AP exam.  
**How to use:** The formula is: `((New − Old) / Old) × 100`. Presets provide real environmental data (population change, deforestation rates, emission reductions). Practice until you can calculate without a calculator — the AP exam provides one, but speed matters.

### 8. Pollution Pathways (`pollution`)
**Icon:** CloudRain  
**Purpose:** Trace 8 pollution source-to-impact pathways for air, water, and land pollution.  
**How to use:** Each pathway shows the pollutant, its source, transport mechanism, environmental impact, and solution. Key distinction: point source (single identifiable source like a factory pipe) vs. nonpoint source (diffuse, like agricultural runoff). The AP exam always includes at least one pollution pathway FRQ.

### 9. Energy & Math (`energy`)
**Icon:** Zap  
**Purpose:** Compare 10 energy sources with their advantages, disadvantages, environmental impacts, and math connections.  
**How to use:** The energy comparison matrix covers fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas), nuclear, and renewables (solar, wind, hydro, geothermal, biomass, hydrogen fuel cells). For each source, study: efficiency, environmental impact, land use, reliability, and cost. The AP exam often asks you to compare two sources and justify a recommendation.

### 10. Solutions & Tradeoffs (`solutions`)
**Icon:** Scale  
**Purpose:** Study 12 environmental solutions with their mechanisms, advantages, disadvantages, unintended consequences, and stakeholder tradeoffs.  
**How to use:** For every solution, practice completing this sentence: "This solution works by [mechanism], which [advantage], but [disadvantage], and may unintentionally [unintended consequence]." Stakeholder tradeoff analysis is essential for FRQ "justify" and "evaluate" prompts.

### 11. FRQ Visual Trainer (`frq`)
**Icon:** PenLine  
**Purpose:** Practice AP-style free-response questions with model answers, weak answers, and scoring guidance.  
**How to use:** Each prompt shows the task verb (identify, describe, explain, evaluate, justify, propose), a model strong answer, a common weak answer, and why the weak answer fails. Focus on task verbs — "identify" requires a single correct term, while "explain" requires a mechanism with cause and effect.

### 12. Misconception Lab (`misconceptions`)
**Icon:** AlertTriangle  
**Purpose:** Confront and correct the 21 most common AP exam misconceptions.  
**How to use:** Each misconception shows the wrong statement, why it's tempting, the correct statement, and an AP-safe sentence you can use on the exam. Review these the night before — eliminating one misconception can save 3–4 points on the MCQ section.

### 13. Night-Before Cram (`cram`)
**Icon:** Moon  
**Purpose:** The 50 highest-yield visual concepts with one must-know sentence and one AP trap each.  
**How to use:** Go through all 50 cards sequentially. For each one, say the must-know sentence aloud, then check yourself against the AP trap. If you can't articulate the distinction, click into the full card. This mode is designed for the final 2–3 hours before the exam.

## Anatomy of a Topic Card (10 Sections)

Every topic card in the atlas contains exactly 10 content sections, designed to cover everything you need for that topic on the AP exam.

### Section 1: Must-Know Concept
**What it is:** A single sentence capturing the essential testable concept for this topic.  
**How to use it:** If you can say this sentence from memory, you understand the topic at a basic level. This is your "floor" — you need at least this for MCQ questions.  
**Example (Topic 1.6 — Carbon Cycle):** "The carbon cycle moves carbon through the atmosphere (CO₂), biosphere (photosynthesis/respiration), hydrosphere (dissolved CO₂), and lithosphere (fossil fuels, limestone) — human combustion of fossil fuels is accelerating the transfer from lithosphere to atmosphere."

### Section 2: Diagram Description
**What it is:** A text description of the key diagram or visual representation for this topic.  
**How to use it:** Mentally reconstruct this diagram. If you were handed a blank piece of paper, could you sketch the essential elements? The AP exam often shows a diagram and asks you to interpret it.

### Section 3: Specific Examples
**What it is:** 3–5 real-world examples with named organisms, locations, numbers, and dates.  
**How to use it:** AP FRQs reward specific examples over generic ones. "DDT biomagnified in bald eagles, thinning eggshells" scores higher than "chemicals build up in food chains." Memorize at least 2 examples per topic.

### Section 4: Causes
**What it is:** The scientific or human causes that drive this environmental phenomenon.  
**How to use it:** When an FRQ says "explain why," it's asking for causes and mechanisms. Each cause should connect to the effect through a clear mechanism.

### Section 5: Effects
**What it is:** The environmental, ecological, or health consequences of the phenomenon.  
**How to use it:** Effects are the "so what" — they connect causes to real impacts. The AP exam frequently asks "describe ONE effect of [phenomenon] on [ecosystem/population/human health]."

### Section 6: Human Impacts
**What it is:** How human activities cause, worsen, or are affected by this environmental issue.  
**How to use it:** Nearly every FRQ includes a human component. Distinguish between "humans caused this" (e.g., fossil fuel combustion → climate change) and "this affects humans" (e.g., sea level rise → coastal flooding).

### Section 7: Solutions
**What it is:** Proposed or implemented solutions with their mechanisms.  
**How to use it:** Solutions must be specific and include a mechanism. "Reduce pollution" earns zero points. "Install scrubbers on coal-fired power plants to remove SO₂ from flue gas before it enters the atmosphere, reducing acid rain formation" earns full credit.

### Section 8: Graph Connection
**What it is:** The type of graph associated with this topic, including axes, trend, and interpretation.  
**How to use it:** The AP exam includes ~6–8 graph-interpretation questions. For each topic, know: What type of graph? What are the axes? What does the trend mean? What calculation might you need to perform?

### Section 9: FRQ Use
**What it is:** How this topic typically appears in AP free-response questions, with task verbs.  
**How to use it:** This tells you the specific question style to expect. Practice writing answers using the exact task verb: identify (name it), describe (what happens), explain (why/how it happens), evaluate (weigh pros and cons), justify (defend a position with evidence).

### Section 10: MCQ Trap / Common Mistake
**What it is:** The most common wrong answer choice or misconception for this topic.  
**How to use it:** If you know the trap, you won't fall for it. These traps are based on the most frequently missed questions from released AP exams. Read each one and ask: "Would I have fallen for this?"

## Search and Filter

### Search Bar
- Searches across: topic title, must-know concept, specific examples, and topic ID
- Real-time filtering as you type
- Works in every mode except Everything Map and Night-Before Cram (which show all cards)

### Unit Filter
- Dropdown to filter by any single AP unit (Unit 1–9) or show all
- Combines with search: e.g., filter to Unit 8 + search "oxygen" → finds Eutrophication and Thermal Pollution

---

## Side Panel

Clicking any topic card anywhere in the atlas opens a detail side panel on the right side of the screen. The side panel shows all 10 sections of the topic card in full, allowing you to study in depth without leaving your current view.

- Click the card → panel opens
- Click the X or click outside → panel closes
- The panel scrolls independently of the main content

## Study Strategies by Time Available

### 1 Week Out
1. Start in **By AP Unit** mode — read every card for Units 5, 6, and 9 (highest exam weight).
2. Switch to **Cycles** mode and master all 4 biogeochemical cycles.
3. Do 10 **FRQ Visual Trainer** prompts per day.
4. Review **Misconception Lab** for units you've covered.

### 3 Days Out
1. Focus on **Cause & Effect** chains — trace all 20, speaking aloud.
2. **Graphs & Data** — practice interpreting each of the 8 graph families.
3. **Energy & Math** — memorize the energy source comparison matrix.
4. **Percent Change Lab** — do 10 practice calculations.

### Night Before
1. **Night-Before Cram** mode — go through all 50 high-yield concepts.
2. **Misconception Lab** — final pass through all 21 misconceptions.
3. **Solutions & Tradeoffs** — review the 12 solutions (FRQs almost always ask about a solution).
4. Sleep. Seriously. A rested brain retrieves information better than a crammed one.

### Morning Of
1. Skim the **Everything Map** — visual overview to activate retrieval.
2. Review the 8 confusable pairs (see VISUAL_ACCURACY_AUDIT.md).
3. Take three deep breaths. You've got this.

### Key Constants
- **9 units** with CED-aligned topic ranges
- **5 big ideas:** ERT (Energy Transfer), ENG (Energy and Ecosystems), SYI (Systems and Interactions), STB (Sustainability), EIN (Environmental Impacts)
- **7 science practices:** Concept Explanation, Visual Representations, Text Analysis, Scientific Experiments, Data Analysis, Mathematical Routines, Environmental Solutions
- **10 visual types:** hierarchy, cycle, cause_effect, source_pathway, cross_section, comparison, graph, percent_change, simulation, solution_tradeoff

### Validation
All data is validated at runtime using Zod schemas defined in `src/lib/enviro/types.ts`. The schemas enforce:
- Required fields for every topic card (id, unit, topicNumber, topicTitle, cedLearningObjective, etc.)
- Enum validation for examWeightRelevance (high/medium/low) and confidence (high/medium/low)
- Array validation for specificExamples, causes, effects, humanImpacts, solutions
- Nested object validation for graphConnection
